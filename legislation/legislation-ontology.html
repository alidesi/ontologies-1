ActOfParliamentc back to ToC or Class ToC

IRI: http://parliament.uk/ontologies/legislation/ActOfParliament

An Act of Parliament creates a new law or changes an existing law. Once a Bill has been approved by Parliament and been given Royal Assent by the Monarch it becomes an Act of Parliament. Taken together, Acts of Parliament which are in force make up what is known as statute law (or the statute book) in the UK.
is in domain of
delegatesop, specifiesDayCountop

DayCountc back to ToC or Class ToC

IRI: http://parliament.uk/ontologies/legislation/DayCount

Some Acts of Parliament define a day count during which Parliament may scrutinise an instrument. The days may either be sitting days or statutory days (see the time period ontology) e.g. the Statutory Instruments Act 1946 defines a default period of 40 statutory days of scrutiny for instruments subject to parliamentary procedure. Sometimes the enabling act (via enabling power) may define an exception to this default. Most commonly this will be 28, 60 or 120 days. The European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018 defines a default period of 10 sitting days of scrutiny for proposed negative statutory instruments.
is in domain of
countdp, dayTypeop
is in range of
dayCountDefinedByop, specifiesDayCountop

DraftAndMadeStatutoryInstrumentSubjectToParliamentaryProcedurec back to ToC or Class ToC

IRI: http://parliament.uk/ontologies/legislation/DraftAndMadeStatutoryInstrumentSubjectToParliamentaryProcedure

Many draft and made statutory instruments are subject to parliamentary procedure. See the procedure ontology for details. This is at a higher conceptual level than the FRBR work identified by The National Archives. It provides a way to abstract away from their document based model towards things that Parliament takes an interest in. It also provides a way to group statutory instrument papers in Parliament in a way that is agnostic to whether they are made or in draft. The distinction made by The National Archives between made statutory instruments (legislation) and draft statutory instruments (draft legislation) is maintained by the mappings to the DraftStatutoryInstrument and MadeStatutoryInstrument classes.

DraftStatutoryInstrumentc back to ToC or Class ToC

IRI: http://parliament.uk/ontologies/legislation/DraftStatutoryInstrument

A draft statutory instrument. Unless Parliament objects, this can become an item of secondary legislation if and when a government minister 'makes' it (signs it into law). Despite not (yet) being legislation, draft statutory instruments are published by The National Archives on legislation.gov.uk. Equivalent to a FRBR work in a legislation.gov.uk sense. Note that draft statutory instruments do not have a full 'SI number' (citation) but do have an ISBN. A draft statutory instrument published on legislation.gov.uk will have a URI derived from this ISBN. If and when a draft statutory instrument is made, it will be assigned a new URI derived from its full SI number.
is in range of
embodiedAsDraftStatutoryInstrumentop

EnablingPowerc back to ToC or Class ToC

IRI: http://parliament.uk/ontologies/legislation/EnablingPower

A power delegated by one or more items of primary legislation, enabling a government minister to bring forward secondary legislation. The terms under which the secondary legislation can be brought forward such as the procedure it will be subject to, any override of statutory day count, and the scope of the secondary legislation are specified by the enabling power.

ProposedNegativeStatutoryInstrumentc back to ToC or Class ToC

IRI: http://parliament.uk/ontologies/legislation/ProposedNegativeStatutoryInstrument

All regulations made under the powers in clauses 7, 8 or 9 of the European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018 which Ministers consider need to be made under the negative procedure will be laid before both Houses as proposed negative statutory instruments. Sifting committees in both Houses may recommend that any subsequent statutory instrument(s) be instead laid under the affirmative procedure. A proposed statutory instrument may lead to a statutory instrument being laid, or multiple, or none.

StatutoryInstrumentSeriesc back to ToC or Class ToC

IRI: http://parliament.uk/ontologies/legislation/StatutoryInstrumentSeries

Statutory instruments may be part of zero, one or many series. Series are commencement and/or appointed day orders which bring into force an Act or part of an Act; legal series which relate to fees or procedures in courts in England and Wales; the Scottish series of instruments made by the United Kingdom Government which apply to Scotland only; the Northern Ireland series of Orders in Council made by the United Kingdom Government; and National Assembly for Wales series of statutory instruments made by the National Assembly for Wales. A series has a label which would be modelled using the preferredLabel from the core ontology and an abbreviation which would be modelled using the abbreviationLabel also from the core ontology.
is in range of
inSeriesop

StatutoryInstrumentSeriesItemc back to ToC or Class ToC

IRI: http://parliament.uk/ontologies/legislation/StatutoryInstrumentSeriesItem

Provides a way to link a draft or made statutory instrument subject to parliamentary procedure to all statutory instrument series of which it is part. Holds the identifier of the SI within a series. A statutory instrument can be an item in more than one series with the following restrictions: Northern Ireland series SIs can't be in any other series; National Assembly for Wales series SIs can also be commencement orders only; Scottish series SIs can also be commencement orders only; legal series SIs can't be part of any other series; and although commencement series documents can also be part of the Welsh or Scottish series they can't be part of both. Where a statutory instrument is an item in more than one series, the citation order is the commencement series and item identifier followed by any other series and item identifier, except where the SI is made by the Welsh Ministers, in which case the 'W. / Cy.' number will always immediately follow the SI number; for example '2005 No. 71 (W. 9) (C. 3)' See also: http://www.legislation.gov.uk/pdfs/StatutoryInstrumentPractice_5th_Edition.pdf#page=65.
is in domain of
inSeriesop, siSeriesItemIdentifierdp
is in range of
isSeriesItemop

WorkPackagec back to ToC or Class ToC

IRI: http://parliament.uk/ontologies/legislation/WorkPackage

A package of work grouping together individual business items e.g. the business items that make up the passage of a specific statutory instrument, or the passage of a specific public bill. Whereas a procedure describes the process, the work package handles the progress of that process for a specific instance. See also the procedure ontology.

delegatesop back to ToC or Object Property ToC

IRI: http://parliament.uk/ontologies/legislation/delegates

Relates an Act of Parliament to an enabling power delegated (at least in part) by that Act. An Act of Parliament might contribute to many enabling powers, an enabling power might be derived from many Acts of Parliament. Secondary legislation can also delegate powers to make further subordinate legislation. This is known as a sub-delegation or tertiary law. Such law is not usually made by statutory instrument, and doesn’t come near Parliament.

specifiesDayCountop back to ToC or Object Property ToC

IRI: http://parliament.uk/ontologies/legislation/specifiesDayCount

Relates an Act of Parliament to a day count. For statutory instruments the default statutory day count is 40 statutory days as defined by the Statutory Instruments Act 1946. Sometimes the enabling Act will override this value. A day count will only ever be derived from one Act of Parliament. It is not possible for a statutory instrument to be enabled by multiple Acts of Parliament where those Acts specify conflicting day counts. It's possible that a single Act of Parliament will specify different day counts in different sections.
has domain
ActOfParliamentc
has range
DayCountc

siYeardp back to ToC or Data Property ToC

IRI: http://parliament.uk/ontologies/legislation/siYear

The year of a statutory instrument. Usually this will be the year it was laid before Parliament. Occasionally a statutory instrument laid in draft in one calendar year and made the following calendar year will have its SI year updated. The year forms the second part of the standard citation 'number' for a statutory instrument e.g. SI 1986/925. Modelled as a literal to add flexibility and because we don't intend to use it mathematically or for ordering.
